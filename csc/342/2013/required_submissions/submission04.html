Overview

This week your task is to write a program that produces, and displays on the standard output or writes to a file, a "concordance" for a file of text.

The Oxford American Dictionary defines "concordance" like this: "An index of the important words used in a book or an author's writings." Our use of the term will be somewhat more liberal. Here is our meaning: "A concordance is a list (in alphabetical order) of all words contained in a file, with each word on a separate line and followed by a list (in increasing numerical order) of all line numbers of the lines of the file in which the word appears." Concordances are very useful to writers when they are preparing indexes for their books, for example.

We also have a very particular meaning for the term "word" in this context. A "word" will be any contiguous string of alphabetic characters, i.e., any contiguous string of upper case and/or lower case letters. Thus, for example, what we would normally think of as the single word "can't" contains, in this context, two "words", namely "can" and "t". Among other things, this means that our concordance program views some sequences of letters as "words" that would not be considered words in the ordinary everyday sense of the term.

Also, all words are considered to be in lower case. Thus, in our concordance, "John", "john" and "JOHN" would all be regarded as the same word, namely "john".

  3. Now try the program on several textfiles of widely varying size and content to get a good feel for the kind of output produced.

  5. Revise your solution until you are convinced that a program that implements your pseudocode will be the one you want.
  6. Translate your pseudocode into C++ and continue testing your program until you are convinced it works correctly.
